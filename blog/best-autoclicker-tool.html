What It Does

At its core, OP Auto Clicker repeatedly triggers mouse clicks based on settings you define, so you don't have to physically click yourself. Once configured, it runs in the background and keeps clicking until you tell it to stop.

Key Features

  • Adjustable click interval — set the delay between clicks down to milliseconds, so you can dial in anything from a slow, occasional click to as fast as your system can handle.
  • Multiple click types — choose single, double, or triple clicks, and pick between left, right, or middle mouse button.
  • Two positioning modes — click wherever your cursor currently sits ("dynamic" mode), or lock onto a fixed set of coordinates on screen ("fixed" mode) so clicks always land in the same spot regardless of where your mouse moves.
  • Click count or infinite repeat — run for a set number of clicks, or let it continue indefinitely until you stop it manually.
  • Customizable hotkeys — start and stop the clicker with a keyboard shortcut (F6 by default) without needing to click back into the app window, so it keeps working even while another program is focused.
  • Saved settings — your interval, click type, and position settings persist between sessions, so you don't have to reconfigure every time you open it.

How to Use It

After downloading, just run the executable — there's no installer, so it opens straight into a small control window. From there:

  • Set your click interval (hours, minutes, seconds, and milliseconds fields are all adjustable independently).
  • Choose your click type (single, double, or triple) and mouse button.
  • Pick "current location" mode, or switch to "fixed location" and use the on-screen picker to set exact coordinates.
  • Decide whether to click a fixed number of times or until stopped.
  • Hit start, or press your configured hotkey to toggle it on and off at any time.

Who It's For

OP Auto Clicker fits anyone who needs simple, repetitive clicking automated without the overhead of a full macro-recording suite. That covers gamers automating idle or grinding mechanics, QA testers running repeated UI interactions, and anyone clicking through the same on-screen button hundreds of times in a row. If you need to record and replay complex multi-step mouse paths across different screen regions, you'll want something with full macro recording — but for straight repetitive clicking, this covers it.

A Quick Word on Safety

Many games and platforms treat automated input as a violation of their terms of service, and some anti-cheat systems flag autoclicker processes outright. Before using one, check whether the software you intend to use it with permits automation. Also, only download from the official site linked above to avoid bundled adware from third-party mirrors.
